It's one of the most common questions we hear from parents in McKinney: *"Is my child old enough for lessons yet?"* It's a good question — and the honest answer is that age is only part of it. Readiness has more to do with attention, interest, and fit than a number on a birthday cake.

Here's how we think about it.

## Age is a starting point, not a rule

There are rough guidelines, and they're useful. Very young children — roughly ages 2 to 6 — usually do best in early-childhood music classes that focus on rhythm, movement, singing, and basic patterns rather than a specific instrument. That groundwork makes picking up guitar, piano, or drums much easier later.

Formal instrument lessons tend to click somewhere around age 6 or 7, once a child can sit and focus for a short stretch and follow a few steps in order. But we've taught kids who were ready earlier and kids who needed another year — and both turned out fine. Age tells you where to start looking, not whether your child is ready.

## What actually matters more than age

When parents ask us about readiness, these are the things we pay attention to:

- **Interest.** Does your child gravitate toward music — humming, tapping, asking about instruments? Genuine curiosity carries a student through the slow early weeks better than anything else.
- **Attention span.** A beginner lesson doesn't require an hour of focus. If your child can stay engaged for 15–20 focused minutes, that's plenty to start.
- **Follows simple steps.** Can they do a two- or three-step task — "put this finger here, count to four, then play"? That's the core mechanic of an early lesson.
- **Willingness to practice a little.** Not a lot. Consistency beats length every time. Ten focused minutes most days will move a student forward faster than one long weekend session.

Notice that none of these are about talent. Talent is real, but it's not the thing that determines whether a child is *ready* — it's the thing that shows up later, once the habit is in place.

## The instrument matters too

Some instruments are simply more forgiving for younger or smaller hands. Ukulele and piano are gentle entry points — the ukulele because it's small and only has four strings, and the piano because every note is laid out in front of you and sounds good the moment you press it. Guitar and drums work beautifully too, sometimes with scaled-down instruments to fit smaller frames.

If you're not sure which instrument fits your child, that's a conversation we're happy to have. Often the right answer is simply whichever one your child is most excited to try.

## When in doubt, start and see

Here's the thing we tell parents most often: you don't have to be certain before you begin. A few lessons will tell you far more than any checklist. If the timing isn't right, that becomes clear quickly and without much lost — and if it *is* right, you'll see the spark almost immediately.

Our number one rule has always been simple: if it sounds good to your child and makes them happy, then it's good. Everything else — the technique, the reading, the progress — grows from that.
