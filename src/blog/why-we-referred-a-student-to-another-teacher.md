Not long ago, a mom came into the studio looking for lessons for her 15-year-old son. He'd had some lessons before, but he was mostly self-taught, extremely motivated, and interested in one thing above all: jazz piano.

We knew right away this wasn't a typical inquiry. Most students who walk through our door are starting from "I'm new and I want to learn how to play" — and we love those students. But every so often, someone shows up with needs that are far more specific. When that happens, the honest question isn't *"How do we sign this student up?"* It's *"What does this student actually need — and are we the right people to provide it?"*

## First, we met him

We didn't try to answer that question over the phone. We asked his mom to bring him in so we could sit down together, hear him play, and understand his goals. There's no substitute for that conversation — a fifteen-minute meeting tells you more than any intake form.

What we learned confirmed the hunch. Jazz piano is its own world: heavy improvisation, advanced chord voicings, and techniques that take years to develop. Our piano instruction is strong — our lead piano instructor is classically trained — but jazz at the level this student was chasing simply isn't what we specialize in. Saying otherwise would have been good for our enrollment numbers and bad for him.

## What we did instead

We didn't just hand his mom a name and wish them luck. We proposed a plan.

There are foundational things we believe every piano player should know, regardless of style — so the student stayed with us for about a month while we worked on those. In the meantime, we reached out to our circle of schools, teachers, and programs to find the right long-term fit.

After talking with several resources, we settled on an instructor who had been teaching jazz piano for more than fifteen years and offered in-home lessons. We facilitated the first meeting between the two of them, and they hit it off immediately — frankly, we were blown away by how good this instructor was.

Then the four of us — the mom, the student, the new instructor, and our studio — agreed on the handoff together. After that first month of foundational work, the new instructor took over. No gap, no guesswork, no starting from scratch.

## Why we work this way

We call it our open-door principle: a family never gets a flat "no" from us. If we're not the right fit, our job becomes finding who is — and making the transition smooth.

That might sound like a strange way to run a business. We sent a motivated, paying student to someone else. But our whole approach to teaching is built on the *why* — why the music works, why the technique matters, why a student practices in the first place. It would be hollow to teach that way and then ignore the biggest *why* of all: why a student is here, and what they actually need to grow.

A student in the right room progresses. A student in the wrong room stalls, no matter how good the teacher is. We'd rather be the studio that put him in the right room.
